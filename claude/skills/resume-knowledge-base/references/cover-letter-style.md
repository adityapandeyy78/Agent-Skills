# Cover Letter Style — Simple Indian Student English, Not AI-Sounding

The cover letter must read like a real final-year Indian student wrote it
herself — genuine, clear, a little eager, never robotic. A recruiter who reads
100 AI letters should feel this one is human.

## Voice
- **Simple, everyday English.** Short sentences. Common words. A student's natural
  register — polite and warm, not corporate or flowery.
- **First person, honest.** "I", "my". It's okay to show real interest and a bit
  of personality. Not over-formal, not slangy.
- **Specific over generic.** Name the company, the role, one real thing about the
  company she likes, and one or two concrete things she has actually done (from
  candidate-profile.md). Specifics are what make it feel human.

## Hard "don't sound like AI" rules
Avoid the tells that scream LLM:
- **Banned openers:** "I am writing to express my interest in...", "I am excited
  to apply for...", "I hope this letter finds you well."
- **Banned phrases:** "I am confident that my skills and experiences...",
  "delve", "leverage", "synergy", "passionate about leveraging", "tapestry",
  "in today's fast-paced world", "I believe I would be a perfect fit", "honed",
  "spearheaded", "myriad", "testament", "align seamlessly", "robust", "moreover",
  "furthermore", "in conclusion".
- **No em-dashes (—).** Use commas or full stops. (AI overuses em-dashes.)
- **No three-item rule-of-three lists in every sentence.** Vary the rhythm.
- **No empty superlatives:** "world-class", "cutting-edge", "passionate",
  "dynamic", "results-driven".
- **Don't restate the whole resume.** Pick 1–2 stories and tell them simply.
- Light, natural imperfection is fine (a normal connecting word, a plain
  sentence). Perfectly balanced corporate prose is the AI tell.

## Structure (keep it ~250–320 words, 3–4 short paragraphs)
1. **Opening (2–3 sentences):** Say the role she's applying for and why this
   company specifically. Mention one concrete, true thing about the company or
   product. No "I am writing to...".
2. **Body para 1:** One real story from her experience that fits the role (e.g.,
   for Data Analyst: the 10K+ records ETL work; for Founder's Office: working with
   the founder on strategy/monetization). What she did and what came out of it,
   in plain words.
3. **Body para 2 (optional):** A second short proof point — a project, case study,
   or leadership example — and how it connects to what the role needs.
4. **Closing (2–3 sentences):** Why she wants to grow in this role/team, a simple
   thank-you, and a light call to talk further. Sign off "Warm regards," /
   "Sincerely," + name.

## Role alignment
Read role-playbooks.md for the role and mirror the JD's priorities in plain
language (don't copy keyword lists into prose — weave 2–3 naturally). Match the
story to the role: don't pitch content-writing for a Data Analyst seat.

## Honesty
Same rule as everywhere: only facts from candidate-profile.md or what the user
says. If the letter needs a detail she hasn't given (why this company, a metric),
ask — don't invent a reason or a number.

## Mini example of the right texture (do not copy verbatim)
> Dear Hiring Team,
>
> I am applying for the Data Analyst role at Acme. I have been following how Acme
> uses pricing data to help small sellers, and that is exactly the kind of work I

> pull more than 10,000 product records and cleaned them for analysis. From that
> data I found seasonal trends that helped shape pricing decisions, and seeing my
> analysis actually change a decision is what got me hooked on this field.
